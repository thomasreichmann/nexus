# Tester Onboarding Script

**Use:** Run this for tester #1's (and every subsequent tester's) first synchronous session. ~30 minutes, 1-on-1, video call or in person.

**Pre-commitment:** This script exists so that founder enthusiasm and demo-instinct don't contaminate the data. The hardest rule for engineers: **shut up and watch.**

## Why this is hard

Most founders ruin their first onboarding sessions by talking too much, fixing things in real time, celebrating when the user figures something out, and apologizing for bugs. All of these contaminate the data. The whole point is to see the product through cold eyes — and the only way to preserve cold eyes is to behave like a researcher, not a guide.

## Pre-session prep

- Confirm tester has brought a real archive — ideally 50GB+ of files they care about, not test files
- Their Nexus account either pre-created or ready to create (don't burn 10 minutes on email-confirmation flows)
- Stripe test card numbers handy in case they hit checkout
- Recording set up (with consent)
- Notepad open for verbatim quotes
- Your fix-it instinct disabled. You will see things break. That is data, not a problem.

## Opening (3 min)

Say roughly this:

> "Today I want to watch you use Nexus for the first time. I'm going to explain almost nothing on purpose — I want to see what's confusing, what's surprising, and where you get stuck. The goal is to get one of your real archive folders uploaded by the end. If something breaks, we just observe it. There are no wrong moves, and you can't hurt anything. Ready?"

Establish ground rules:

- They drive. You don't touch the keyboard.
- You'll be quiet on purpose. Their silence is fine too.
- If they get stuck, you'll wait at least 60 seconds before stepping in.

## Tasks (25 min)

Give one at a time. Do **not** present them as a list.

### Task 1: Sign up and reach the upload screen

Watch:

- Do they understand the plan tiers? Did they pick correctly?
- Did Stripe checkout in test mode confuse them?
- Where did they hesitate?

### Task 2: Upload a real folder

(Wedding, event — at least 50GB.)

Watch:

- Drag-and-drop, or hunt for a button?
- Did they assume folder structure was preserved?
- What did they expect to see during upload?
- If upload is slow, _do not fill the silence_. Let them experience the wait.

### Task 3: Find the upload after it completes

> "Now show me what you just uploaded."

Watch:

- Do they understand the batch view?
- Can they tell what's there?
- Did they expect to see thumbnails?

### Task 4: Initiate a restore

> "Pretend a client just emailed asking for one specific file from this wedding. Get it back."

Watch:

- How do they pick the file/batch?
- Do they understand restore takes hours?
- Are they surprised by the tier choices?

### Task 5: Future use (if time)

> "When's your next shoot? Walk me through what you'd do with those files."

This reveals whether they are mentally placing Nexus in the workflow or treating this as a one-off test.

## Closing (5 min)

Three questions, in this order:

1. What surprised you?
2. What confused you?
3. If you had to explain Nexus to another photographer in one sentence, what would you say?

Set expectations:

- "I'll check in weekly. The question I'll ask is: _Did you shoot anything this week? Did it end up in Nexus? If not, where?_ That's it. No homework."
- "When's your next shoot? Will it land in Nexus?" _(Get a verbal commitment. Note their hesitation.)_

## Founder rules — do NOT do these

- Don't pre-explain what Nexus is. Let them experience it.
- Don't take the keyboard, ever, even to "just show them quickly."
- Don't celebrate when they figure something out ("yes! exactly!"). Stay neutral.
- Don't apologize for bugs. Note them; move on.
- Don't context-switch to a fix mid-session.
- Don't add follow-up questions that reveal the answer you want.
- Don't promise features in response to wishes. "Tell me more about that" — never "we'll add that."

## Post-session (within 1 hour, while it's fresh)

- Write up verbatim notes
- List 3 things that surprised you
- List any bugs/blockers — but **do not fix them yet.** Wait until you've watched 2-3 testers do the same flow. A "bug" the first tester hits might be an "obvious workflow" the next tester completes effortlessly. You can't tell which until you have multiple data points.
